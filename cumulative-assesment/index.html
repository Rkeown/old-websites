Safety

An example would be a power supply

Some electrical devices have certain power requirements

  • An example would be AC adapters for laptops, most adapters are designed for their intended device and using a different one could cause damage to the device

Electrical devices must be grounded

  • Grounding provides a path of least resistance for the current to flow through and away from you and your equipement

Electrostatic Discharge (ESD) can occur when a buildup of electric charge in a surface comes in contact with a differently charged surface.
ESD can cause major damage to computer components if not discharged safely, mainly internal components. The minimum volts required before a human can feel a shock is around 3,000 volts, this can be achieved by simply walking on carpet with socks on. On the other hand computer internals need less 30 volts

Some safety recommendations to follow to avoid ESD are:

  1. Keep all internals in anti static bags until they are ready to be installed
  2. If possible use anti static mats and workbenches
  3. Use antistatic wrist straps

Cases

cases are basically what hold everything that your pc needs to run

Cases come in different form factors

  • Horizontal tower
  • Full size tower
  • Compact tower
  • All in one

Cases are typically made of plastic, steel, or aluminum and provide and provide the framework to support, protect, and cool the internal components (Many case manufacturers may have their own naming conventions, including super tower, full tower, mid tower, mini tower, cube case, and more).
Computer components tend to generate a lot of heat, and therfore cases contain fans that move air through the case.

As air passes warm components, it absors the heat and then exits the case. This Keeps the components from overheating. Cases also protect the internal components from ESD by being the ground for them via attachement to the case

Power supplies

Computers use a power supply to convert AC power into a lower voltage DC power required by internal components


Desktop computer power supply form factors include:

  • Advanced technology (AT)
    The original power supply for legacy systems
    Now considred obsolete
  • AT Extended (ATX)
    Updated version of AT
    Still obsolete...
  • ATX12V
    The most common power supply of right now
    Includes second motherboard connecter for giving dedicated power to the CPU
    Several versions of ATX12V exist
  • ESP12V
    Originally designed for network servers
    Now used in top of the line desktops

Connectors
